Metacognition

  • Knowledge and understanding of one's own thinking.

  • Awareness or analysis of one's own learning or thinking processes.

  • Awareness of one's own cognitive processes, often involving a conscious attempt to control them.


    • Components

  • Metacognitive knowledge.
  • Metacognitive experience.
  • Metacognitive monitoring and regulation.


    • Metacognitive Knowledge

  • Personal knowledge.

    • (hobbies, memory characteristics, ways of thinking, ability limitations)

  • Task knowledge.

    • (task structures, task goals)

  • Strategic knowledge.

    • (advantages, disadvantages, applicability of strategies)


    Metacognitive Experience

    An individual's subjective perception of the level of difficulty of particular cognitive operations such as that of remembering, learning, language use, problem solving, decision making, reasoning.


    Monitoring & Regulation

    It is the ability to plan, monitor, and evaluate one's own cognitive activities and regulate cognitive processes such as adjusting task goals, distributing learning time, selecting cognitive strategies. This helps improve one's own performance in many ways such as better use of attentional resources and existing strategies, a greater awareness of comprehension breakdowns.

    • Schema

  • A drawing that represents an idea or theory and makes it easier to understand.

  • A collection of basic knowledge about a concept or entity that serves as a guide to perception, interpretation, imagination, or problem solving.

  • A mental codification of experience that includes a particular organized way of perceiving cognitively and responding to a complex situation or set of stimuli.

  • A cognitive structure representing a person's knowledge about some entity or situation, including its qualities and the relationships between these. Schemas are usually abstractions that simplify a person's world. In 1932, Frederic C. Bartlett showed that past experiences are stored in memory as schemas; impressions of other people are also thought to be organized in this way.


    • Example

    The schema “restaurant” suggests that it may have chairs and tables, that it may or may not have air conditioners, and that it probably will not have beds.


    Types

  • Self schema
  • Object schema
  • Person schema
  • Role schema
  • Social schema
  • Event schema (script)


    • Self Schema

    They help understand oneself. They focus on what one knows about who one is in the present, who one was in the past, and who one could be in the future.


    Object Schema

    They help one understand inanimate objects. For example, you have a schema for what a chair is and how to use it.


    Person Schema

    They help one understand specific people. For example, one's schema for their friend or partner will include their appearances, their likes and dislikes, and other traits.


    Role Schema

    They are related to one's expectations of how a person in a specific social role will behave. For example, one expects a teacher to be wise. While not all teachers may be that way, one's schema sets one's expectations of each teacher one interacts with.


    Social Schema

    They include general information about how to behave in various social situations. For example, if one plans to go to a restaurant, their restaurant schema provides them with a general understanding of the type of social situation to expect when they go to a restaurant.


    Event Schema

    Also known as scripts, they deal with the sequence of actions and behaviours one expects during a specific event. For example, when an individual goes to a restaurant, they anticipate going to the restaurant, reading the menu, talking to the waiter, ordering and eating the food, paying the bill, and then exiting the restaurant.


    Limitations

  • One's schemas can influence or reduce their understanding of novel things.

  • One's schemas can influence their judgment of others' personalities.

  • Scripts do not provide apparatus for novel situations.

  • When one perceives another's personality as stable or consistent, it is likely that the trait exists within the mind of the one judging more than within the behaviour of the person being judged.


    • Dissonance

    New information that cannot be integrated into one's current schemas can create cognitive dissonance.
